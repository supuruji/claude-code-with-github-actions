# Vocabulary Level Selection Guide

## Difficulty Levels

### Beginner (CEFR A1-A2 / TOEIC 200-500)

**Characteristics**:
- High-frequency words used in daily life
- Concrete nouns and common verbs
- Simple adjectives and basic adverbs
- Words with direct, literal meanings

**Examples**: accomplish, benefit, considerable, demonstrate, establish

**Selection Criteria**:
- Appears in top 3,000 most common English words
- Has a clear, single primary meaning
- Easily illustrated with simple examples
- Useful in everyday conversations

---

### Intermediate (CEFR B1-B2 / TOEIC 500-750)

**Characteristics**:
- Words common in business and academic texts
- Abstract concepts and nuanced meanings
- Words with multiple definitions depending on context
- Formal alternatives to everyday words

**Examples**: alleviate, conducive, expedite, mitigate, paramount

**Selection Criteria**:
- Appears in academic or business word lists
- May have 2-3 distinct meanings
- Useful in professional communication
- Commonly tested on TOEIC/TOEFL

---

### Advanced (CEFR C1-C2 / TOEIC 750+)

**Characteristics**:
- Low-frequency but high-impact vocabulary
- Precise technical or academic terms
- Words with subtle connotations
- Sophisticated alternatives showing language mastery

**Examples**: ameliorate, corroborate, exacerbate, pernicious, ubiquitous

**Selection Criteria**:
- Appears in advanced academic texts
- Conveys precise meaning not easily expressed by simpler words
- Common in formal writing, research, and high-level discourse
- Distinguishes advanced speakers from intermediate ones

---

## Part of Speech Distribution

Aim for this approximate distribution in each word set:

| Part of Speech | Target % | Rationale |
|---------------|----------|-----------|
| Nouns | 35-40% | Most testable, easiest to define clearly |
| Verbs | 25-30% | Essential for sentence construction |
| Adjectives | 20-25% | Important for description and nuance |
| Adverbs | 5-10% | Smaller set, but useful for precision |

## Word Selection Tips

1. **Avoid cognates**: Skip words that are identical or near-identical in major languages (e.g., "international", "television") unless the English usage differs significantly.

2. **Prefer versatile words**: Choose words that appear in multiple contexts (business, academic, daily) over domain-specific jargon.

3. **Include word families**: When possible, select words that have productive word families (e.g., "collaborate" gives collaborate/collaboration/collaborative/collaboratively).

4. **Consider testability**: Select words that can generate clear, unambiguous practice questions.

5. **Balance familiar and novel**: Mix some words learners may have encountered but not mastered with completely new items.
